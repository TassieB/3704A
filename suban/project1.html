Project 1 - 7 Stages of Lucidy by Joshua Bach

Introduction

Our knowledge about how consciousness is produced remains limited. However, we do understand that we exist and possess consciousness. The brain operates within a closed environment and derives its perception of the world from sensory input. This reliance on our five senses (smell, touch, vision, hearing, taste) and the brain acts as an intermediary between us and the true reality. Consequently, our experiences are not direct encounters with reality but rather our individual interpretations. Our brain's interpretation of the world is influenced by our fundamental assumptions, which are often developed in early life or during identity crises.

Objective

The primary aim of this webpage is to provide a concise overview of one of the captivating concepts (The Stages of Lucidity) discussed by Joscha Bach in the featured podcast with Lex Fridman. While his theory is influenced by the ideas of psychologist Robert Keegan, Bach integrates his expertise in AI, consciousness, and cognitive science to formulate his unique theory.

The Seven Stages:

Stage 1 (Infancy)

In early infancy, the idea of a distinct self is absent. Instead, an attentional self is at work, gradually shaping our perception of the world and forming the initial self-image. Think of it as the brain constructing its own game engine to process sensory information and make sense of it.

Stage 2 (Young Child)

Upon the completion of the initial world model (stage 1), the notion of self begins to form, serving as an agent that allows us to engage with the surrounding environment. Although we commonly view the external world as an objective reality, it's important to understand that what we experience is, in fact, a mental construct generated by our own minds. Consequently, during early childhood, our interactions with the "external" world are influenced by the world model we developed in stage 1.

Stage 3 (Adolescence)

​​In the third stage of personal growth, people start to shape their social identity. This identity is influenced by something called intuitive empathy, which means building a mental architecture similar to your group. People in this stage develop interests related to their social groups, feel like they're part of a team, and start to adopt the ideas and values they see around them. However, some individuals with high-functioning autism, often referred to as "nerds," may struggle with this intuitive empathy, which makes it harder for them to relate to people who think differently. Even though they care about others and want to fit in, they find it challenging to grasp how others feel. It's essential to realize that this kind of empathy is not just a choice or a way of thinking – it's like a unique sense in our minds. As a result, "nerds" might skip this stage and move directly to the next one, where they discuss ideas and rules logically instead of relying on their emotions. They do this to find their place among others who are similar to them. The encouraging part is that, similar to how we can develop our other senses, intuitive empathy can improve over time, even if it's not very strong initially.

Stage 4 (Rational Agent)

In this stage, you come to understand that the truth doesn't depend on what others believe, and you gain the ability to shape your own beliefs. This is when you explore epistemology, which is the study of how we determine what's true or false. Instead of constantly seeking the opinions of others, you become empowered to independently determine what you consider to be true.

Stage 5 (Self-Athorising)

In this phase, you start to grasp the process of forming your identity. You come to the realization that your values are not fixed endpoints but tools for creating a world you find appealing and a personal style that suits your preferences. The better you comprehend this concept, the more control you have over crafting your identity. You understand that your identity and how you interact with others are like costumes you can put on and take off. These "costumes" are useful because they convey something about you to others and allow you to play various roles, such as being a partner, daughter, employee, boss, or friend. This stage is often considered as full adulthood because you begin to appreciate that people shape their identities based on their past experiences. You come to understand that if you had lived through their experiences, you could be in their shoes, taking on a different role in a separate timeline.

Stage 6 (Enlightenment)

​​While stage 5 involves breaking down your own identity, stage 6 delves even deeper into the way we perceive things. It deals with how we create our sensory experiences, motivation, and how we control our body's functioning. This stage is typically explored by advanced meditators. Stage 6 can lead us to a profound understanding by breaking down the barrier between our personal perspective and our mental processes. We realize that all our experiences, including what we perceive and how we're motivated, are based on our mental representations. Without a logical way of understanding knowledge, it might seem like we're interconnected with and have control over the entire universe, which is how it feels subjectively (the world we perceive is like a simulation created by our own mind). It's possible to learn the techniques of stage 6 without going through stage 5, for example, through guidance from experienced meditators. Stage 6 has the power to significantly transform how we experience reality, freeing us from personal suffering and social inhibitions. It's evident why it can be risky to master stage 6 without first gaining the wisdom of stage 5.

Stage 7 (Transcendence)

This stage is somewhat speculative, as described by Bach. It's a trans-humanist phase where you gain a deep understanding of how your mind operates. You become fully aware of the mechanisms behind your thoughts and can, in theory, explore different ways in which your mind could be implemented. Bach suggests that this stage is not currently accessible to people, but it might become possible in the future if and when we integrate with artificial intelligence.

Conclusion

In the fascinating journey of personal growth through the seven stages, the third stage, adolescence, stands out as a crucial point. It's the phase where we shape our social identity and develop intuitive empathy. However, what's intriguing is that not everyone smoothly moves beyond this stage. Individuals with high-functioning autism, for instance, may find it challenging to navigate this phase and might jump ahead to logical reasoning and idea exploration. This diversity in personal development reminds us that our growth paths are unique. Each stage teaches us something valuable about human development, emphasizing the richness of our individual experiences. The seven stages explained by Joshua Bach in the podcast provides profound insights into the intricate realm of human consciousness.
